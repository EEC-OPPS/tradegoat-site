Switching Software Shouldn't Mean Starting Over

By Travis Evans, Founder of TradeGOAT · 20+ years in the trades

I've watched a lot of contractors try a new piece of software. I've done it myself. Most of the time it ends the same way — a month in, we're back to the old way of doing things.

For a long time I figured that was on us. Too busy, too stuck in our habits. But that isn't the real reason.

It fails because getting started means rebuilding everything you already have.

Think about what a new tool asks you to do on day one. Retype your proposal into their template. Rebuild your invoice in their format. Re-enter your customers, your rates, your terms. All of it — work you already did, in documents that already work — done over again inside somebody else's system.

That isn't setup. That's a second job. And nobody in this trade has time for a second job.

The part that always bothered me

The proposal you send isn't a rough draft. You spent years getting it right. Your customers recognize it, and that recognition is part of why they trust you. The same goes for your invoice, your terms, and the way you describe your work. So why does every tool treat all of that as something to throw out and replace?

I spent twenty years in the elevator trade and four running my own company before I built any of this. When I finally sat down to build TradeGOAT, I started from the opposite idea: your documents are the thing worth keeping, and the software's job is to carry them, not replace them.

How it actually works

You upload the proposal you already send. You pick the customer. Your own document comes back filled in — same wording, same layout, the version your customers already know. There's nothing to rebuild and nothing new to learn.

From there, your office and your crew in the field are looking at the same information at the same time. When a job moves, everyone sees it move. The invoice is ready the day the work is finished, not three days later when someone finally gets to the paperwork.

The real value of your business was never sitting inside a piece of software. It's in your documents, your customers, and the way you've learned to do the work.

A tool worth using should protect that. It shouldn't ask you to trade it away for a fresh start you never wanted.
