Before You Buy Software for Your Trade Business: 5 Questions That Save You Months

By Travis Evans, Founder of TradeGOAT · 20+ years in the trades

First, a truth the software industry doesn't like saying out loud: if you're running your company on Word proposals, a pricing spreadsheet, and QuickBooks — you already have a system, and it works. It won you the business you have. The documents sound like you, and your customers know them.

So when software comes courting, the real question isn't "is this tool good?" It's: does this tool respect the system I already built, or does it make me start over? Most of them make you start over — new templates, new workflows, new habits for everyone including the guys in the truck. You're busy running jobs; the rebuild never finishes; the subscription keeps billing. Six months later you're back in Word, out the money and the time.

I've been on that ride. These are the five questions I wish I'd asked before giving anyone a card number.

1. "Does it use MY documents — or your templates?"

This is the question that sorts the whole market, so ask it first. Your proposal took years to get right. Most tools will answer with a template gallery: pick one of theirs, then spend your evenings moving your business into it, line by line. That's not setup — that's unpaid data entry, and it's exactly the work that kills adoption in month one.

The test: hand the vendor a real proposal you sent last month and ask, "What does this exact document look like coming out of your system?" If the answer isn't "the same — it's your document," you've learned what your first month looks like.

2. "What does my crew have to learn?"

A tool the office loves and the field ignores is a tool half your company isn't using. If a tech needs a training session to clock in or open a work ticket, it won't survive the first busy week. The field side should work like the apps already on his phone — a few big buttons, nothing to memorize.

3. "What does my customer see?"

Some tools put a portal between you and your customer — a login they've never seen, an invoice layout that doesn't look like you, a text from a number they don't know. Your paperwork is part of your reputation; you spent years making it trustworthy. Ask to see exactly what a customer receives — the document, the email, all of it — and whose name and formatting is on every piece.

4. "What's actually included at that price?"

Here's a pattern worth knowing before you shop: the advertised monthly price is usually the doorway, not the house. QuickBooks sync, text messaging, professional proposals — in much of this market those are add-ons, sold separately, on top of the base plan. It's common for a tool advertised at a modest monthly number to land at several times that once a real company turns everything on. Ask for the all-in price, in writing, with every feature you'll actually use switched on — before the trial ends, not after.

5. "What happens to the price when I add a person?"

Per-seat pricing is the quiet one. It sounds harmless at signup, and then it charges you for growing — add a tech in the spring, add your spouse doing the books, and the bill climbs each time. Ask what the price is with every person who'll ever touch the tool counted in. If the number moves every time your company does, you'll spend next year rationing logins and sharing passwords, and that's no way to run a system.

What good looks like: add a tech, add your spouse — your price doesn't move.

One more, for free: "How do I leave?"

You shouldn't need an exit plan for office software, but ask anyway: if this doesn't work out, what do I get back, and how fast can I cancel? A vendor who's confident in the product makes leaving easy. A vendor who makes leaving hard is telling you how they plan to keep you.

The pattern behind all of it

Every question above is the same question wearing different clothes: does the software bend to how your business already works, or do you bend to it? You've spent years figuring out how to run your company. The right tool starts from that. The wrong one costs you a subscription and a rebuild you never had time for — and it charges you extra when you grow.
