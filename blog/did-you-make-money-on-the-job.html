How to Know If You Actually Made Money on a Job — Before, During, and After

By Travis Evans, Founder of TradeGOAT · 20+ years in the trades

Every contractor I know has at least one job they'll never forget for the wrong reason. It looked good going in. The bid was solid, the work went more or less to plan, and then the final numbers landed and the profit just wasn't there. You go back through it trying to figure out where it went, and half the time you can't even find the leak. It's just gone.

I've had those jobs. And for years I told myself what everybody tells themselves: it happens, you win some and lose some, move on to the next one. But "move on" is exactly the problem. If you never go back and figure out where the money went, the next job is set up to lose it in the same spot.

Here's the thing it took me too long to see: a bid is a prediction. It's your best, most careful guess at what a job will cost and what it'll leave you. And most contractors never once check the prediction against what actually happened.

Why the leak stays hidden

It's not that contractors don't care about the numbers. It's that the numbers show up scattered, over weeks, and never land back next to the original bid.

The material that came in higher than you figured. The two extra trips nobody logged. The eight labor hours over what you estimated. The small change the customer asked for that you never billed because it felt too minor to bring up. None of those feel like a disaster on the day they happen. They trickle in, one at a time, and by the time the job closes they've quietly eaten your margin — with no single place where you'd ever see them add up.

So the job "feels" fine, you close it, and you carry the exact same blind spot into the next one.

The fix is three views of the same job

What changed my business was being able to look at a job three ways, all against the same number: the price I set going in.

Before — you priced it right. This is where it starts, and it's why pricing the right way matters so much. A job you priced with a real method gives you a real baseline — the prediction you're going to measure everything against. Price by gut and there's nothing to measure against later.

During — this is the one that saves jobs. The biggest value isn't the autopsy at the end. It's watching the actual costs stack up against the bid while the job is still running. When you can see a job drifting toward the red with two weeks left — instead of finding out after it's closed — you can actually do something about it. Reassign the crew. Have the scope-change conversation before you eat the change for free. Tighten up the trips. You catch it while there's still time to correct.

After — you find the leak, with a number. When the job's done, you hold the finished thing up against the bid and see exactly where it bled. Labor hours? A material you underpriced? Travel you swallowed? You stop guessing at what went wrong and start seeing it in black and white.

And then the loop starts paying you back

Here's where it compounds. Every job you close out this way teaches the next bid. Underbid labor on that last job? The next one gets priced with your eyes open. Keep eating travel on the work across the county? Now you build it in.

Pricing and tracking aren't two separate things — they feed each other. A good price gives you something to measure. Measuring the finished job makes your next price sharper. Do that a hundred times and you're not the same estimator you were a year ago. You're pricing off what really happened, not what you hoped would happen.

That's the whole difference — between being busy and running a business that actually gets better.
