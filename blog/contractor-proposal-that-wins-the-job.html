You Don't Always Lose the Job on Price. Here's What Actually Wins It.

By Travis Evans, Founder of TradeGOAT · 20+ years in the trades

There's a myth in the trades that the low bid wins. Sometimes it does. But I've lost plenty of jobs where I was the cheapest, and won plenty where I wasn't even close. After enough of those, you start to realize the price isn't the whole story. The proposal is.

The proposal is the one thing the customer is actually holding when they decide. It's your pitch, your first impression, and your handshake, all in one document. And most contractors treat it as an afterthought — a number and a "let me know." The ones who win treat it like it matters, because it does.

I've lost jobs I should have won because the other guy's proposal looked like it came from a real company and mine looked like I scribbled it in the truck. And I've won jobs at a higher price because my proposal made the customer feel like they knew exactly what they were getting and that I'd actually show up. It doesn't matter the trade — HVAC, electrical, concrete, roofing, elevators. The proposal is where the job is won or lost. Here's what goes in one that wins.

1. Crystal-clear scope — what's in, and what's not

The number one reason a customer doesn't sign isn't price. It's uncertainty. If they can't tell exactly what they're getting, they stall. So spell it out — what's included, and, just as importantly, what's not.

That clarity does double duty. It wins the job, and it protects you later. Fuzzy scope is how a won job turns into a fight three weeks in: "I thought that was included." A proposal that draws the line clearly up front means you never have that conversation.

2. It has to look like it came from a real business

Fair or not, your proposal is the customer's first read on whether you're a professional or a guy with a truck. A clean, consistent, professional document quietly says, "I run a real business, and I'll run your job the same way." A number on the back of a business card says the opposite — even if you're the best tradesman in town.

You're not just bidding the work. You're auditioning to be trusted in someone's building or someone's home. Look the part.

3. It's about their job, in their words

A customer can smell a generic template a mile away. The proposals that win reflect the actual job — their building, their problem, the specific work in front of you — not a copy-paste with the name swapped out.

That's the difference between "here's a quote" and "here's how I'm going to solve your problem." One is a commodity. The other is the reason they hire you.

4. Make the price make sense

A price with no context is just a number to line up against the other guy's number — and now you're in a race to the bottom you don't want to win. A price attached to clear scope gives the customer something to weigh besides cost.

And where the work could genuinely go two different ways, giving them a real choice — not a gimmicky good-better-best ladder, but honest options that fit the job — lets them buy on value instead of price alone. You stop being a number on a spreadsheet.

5. Make it easy to say yes

The best proposal in the world doesn't help if signing it is a hassle. Clear next step. Easy to sign. No friction. Every extra day and every extra step is one more chance for the customer to cool off, get busy, or call somebody else. Take the work out of hiring you.

6. Speed wins more jobs than people admit

A lot of work goes to whoever gets a solid proposal in front of the customer first — while they still feel the problem. Not the cheapest. Not even the best. The first one who makes it easy to say yes.

If it takes you three days to get a proposal out because you're rebuilding it from scratch every time, you're losing jobs you never even knew were close. Fast and professional beats slow and perfect.
